Specimen Collection and Handling

Critical Pre-analytical Factors

Specimen Types

  • Urogenital: Urine, urethral, cervical, vaginal swabs
  • Extragenital: Pharyngeal, rectal swabs
  • Blood: Serum, plasma, whole blood
  • Lesions: Ulcer swabs, vesicle fluid

Collection Timing

  • Window periods for antibody tests
  • First-void urine for NAAT
  • Active lesions for direct detection
  • Avoid recent urination (≥1 hour)
Important: Improper specimen collection is the most common cause of false-negative results

Transport and Storage

Specimen Type Transport Medium Temperature Maximum Time
NAAT swabs Manufacturer's medium 2-8°C 7 days
Culture swabs Stuart's/Amies Room temp 24 hours
Serum None 2-8°C 7 days

Common Diagnostic Challenges

Technical Challenges

  • Cross-reactivity: False positives in serological tests
  • Sensitivity issues: Early infection, low pathogen load
  • Specimen adequacy: Insufficient cells/material
  • Inhibitors: Substances affecting molecular tests

Clinical Challenges

Patient Factors

  • Recent antibiotic use
  • Immunosuppression
  • Co-infections
  • Anatomical variations

Testing Limitations

  • Window periods
  • Test availability
  • Cost constraints
  • Turnaround time
Solution: Use appropriate testing algorithms and confirmatory tests when indicated

Syphilis (Treponema pallidum) Diagnosis

Overview

  • Causative agent: Treponema pallidum subspecies pallidum
  • Cannot be cultured in routine laboratory
  • Diagnosis relies on direct detection and serology
  • Different tests needed for different stages

Clinical Stages and Testing

Stage Clinical Features Preferred Tests
Primary Chancre (3-90 days) Direct detection + Serology
Secondary Rash, systemic symptoms Serology (high titers)
Latent Asymptomatic Serology only
Tertiary Gummas, cardiovascular, neuro Serology + CSF (if neuro)

Slide 8 of 45

Syphilis: Direct Detection Methods

Direct Detection Techniques

Darkfield Microscopy

  • Requires fresh specimen
  • Experienced microscopist
  • See spirochetes moving
Sensitivity: 80% Specificity: 97%

DFA-TP

  • Fluorescent antibody stain
  • Can use fixed specimens
  • More specific than darkfield
Sensitivity: 90% Specificity: 99%

PCR

  • Most sensitive method
  • Not widely available
  • Research settings
Sensitivity: 95% Specificity: 99%
Note: Direct detection is only useful when lesions are present (primary & secondary syphilis)

Slide 9 of 45

Syphilis: Serological Tests

Two Categories of Serological Tests

Non-treponemal Tests

  • RPR (Rapid Plasma Reagin)
  • VDRL (Venereal Disease Research Lab)
  • Detect antibodies to cardiolipin
  • Quantitative (titer)
  • Monitor treatment response
False Positives: Pregnancy, autoimmune diseases, elderly

Treponemal Tests

  • TPPA/TPHA
  • FTA-ABS
  • EIA/CIA
  • Immunoblots
  • Remain positive for life
  • Confirmatory tests
Advantage: More specific for T. pallidum

Slide 10 of 45

Syphilis Testing Algorithms

Traditional vs Reverse Algorithm

Traditional Algorithm

  1. Screen with RPR/VDRL
  2. If positive → Confirm with TPPA
  3. Report with titer

Pros: Cost-effective, monitors treatment

Cons: May miss early/late syphilis

Reverse Algorithm

  1. Screen with EIA/CIA
  2. If positive → RPR/VDRL
  3. Discordant → TPPA

Pros: Higher sensitivity, automation

Cons: More complex interpretation

Result Interpretation

Treponemal Non-treponemal Interpretation
Positive Positive Active syphilis (confirm stage)
Positive Negative Past treated syphilis OR early/late syphilis
Negative Positive False positive (confirm with another treponemal)

Slide 11 of 45

Syphilis: Treatment Monitoring

Monitoring Treatment Response

Key Principle: Use quantitative non-treponemal tests (RPR/VDRL) to monitor treatment

Expected Response

  • 4-fold decline in titer = successful treatment
  • Check at 6 and 12 months for primary/secondary
  • Check at 6, 12, and 24 months for latent

Treatment Failure Indicators

  • Lack of 4-fold decline by 6-12 months
  • 4-fold increase in titer
  • Clinical signs persist/recur
Remember: Treponemal tests remain positive for life - do NOT use for monitoring

Slide 13 of 45

Gonorrhea: Microscopy and Culture

Gram Stain Microscopy

Procedure

  • Direct smear from discharge
  • Gram stain technique
  • Look for intracellular diplococci
  • Inside polymorphonuclear cells

Performance by Site

  • Male urethra: Sens 95%, Spec 99%
  • Cervix: Sens 50%, Spec 95%
  • Pharynx/Rectum: Not recommended

Culture Methods

Culture Requirements

  • Media: Chocolate agar, Thayer-Martin (selective)
  • Atmosphere: 5-10% CO₂, 35-37°C
  • Transport: Immediate plating or transport medium
  • Identification: Oxidase, catalase, sugar utilization
Critical: Culture enables antimicrobial susceptibility testing - essential for resistance surveillance

Slide 14 of 45

Gonorrhea: Nucleic Acid Testing

NAAT Advantages

  • Superior sensitivity (>95%) compared to culture
  • Can test multiple anatomical sites
  • Stable specimens - no viability required
  • Automated platforms available
  • Co-testing with Chlamydia

Specimen Types for NAAT

Patient Specimen Collection Notes
Males First-void urine First 10-20 mL No urination ≥1 hour
Females Vaginal swab Self or clinician Preferred specimen
All Pharyngeal Posterior pharynx Based on exposure
All Rectal Anal canal Based on exposure
Limitation: NAAT cannot provide antimicrobial susceptibility - culture still needed for resistance testing

Gonorrhea: Antimicrobial Resistance Testing

Global Resistance Crisis

  • Resistance to all first-line antibiotics has emerged
  • Ceftriaxone is last remaining empiric option
  • XDR (extensively drug-resistant) strains reported

Susceptibility Testing Methods

Disk Diffusion

  • Standardized method
  • CLSI guidelines
  • Qualitative results

MIC Testing

  • E-test or agar dilution
  • Quantitative results
  • Required for surveillance
Saudi Recommendation: Culture from treatment failures and perform susceptibility testing

Chlamydia trachomatis Diagnosis

Overview

  • Obligate intracellular bacterium
  • Most common bacterial STI worldwide
  • 70-80% asymptomatic in women
  • Major cause of PID and infertility
  • Includes LGV serovars (L1, L2, L3)

Diagnostic Evolution

Historical: Cell culture (McCoy cells) → Complex, expensive

Transitional: Direct fluorescent antibody, EIA → Lower sensitivity

Current Standard: NAAT → Highly sensitive and specific

Key Point: NAAT is the only recommended method for routine chlamydia diagnosis

Slide 17 of 45

Chlamydia: NAAT Testing

NAAT Performance

  • Sensitivity: >90-95%
  • Specificity: >99%
  • FDA-approved for multiple specimen types
  • Can detect as few as 1-10 organisms

Specimen Selection

Clinical Scenario Preferred Specimen Alternative
Asymptomatic female screening Vaginal swab (self-collected) First-void urine
Symptomatic female Cervical swab Vaginal swab
Male (any) First-void urine Urethral swab
Extragenital Site-specific swab -

Co-testing Advantages

  • Most platforms test for C. trachomatis + N. gonorrhoeae
  • Single specimen, reduced cost
  • High co-infection rates justify dual testing

Slide 18 of 45

Chlamydia: Special Considerations

LGV (Lymphogranuloma Venereum) Testing

  • Caused by C. trachomatis serovars L1, L2, L3
  • Requires genotyping - not available in routine labs
  • Clinical diagnosis + positive chlamydia NAAT
  • Consider in: proctitis, inguinal buboes, genital ulcers

Test of Cure

  • NOT recommended routinely due to dead organism detection
  • Wait ≥4 weeks if testing needed
  • Indicated for:
    • Pregnant women
    • Compliance concerns
    • Persistent symptoms

False Results

False Positives

  • Very rare with NAAT
  • Low prevalence populations
  • Consider confirmatory testing

False Negatives

  • Recent exposure (<7 days)
  • Inhibitors in specimen
  • Improper collection

Trichomoniasis Diagnosis

Overview

  • Causative agent: Trichomonas vaginalis (protozoan)
  • Most common non-viral STI globally
  • Associated with adverse pregnancy outcomes
  • Increases HIV transmission risk
  • Often asymptomatic (70-85%)

Diagnostic Methods Comparison

Method Sensitivity Advantages Limitations
Wet Mount 44-68% Rapid, inexpensive Requires immediate exam
Culture 75-96% Viable organisms 3-7 days, special media
NAAT >95% Highly sensitive Cost, availability
Rapid Antigen 82-95% Point-of-care Moderate sensitivity

Slide 20 of 45

Trichomoniasis: Testing Methods

Wet Mount Microscopy

Procedure

  1. Mix vaginal discharge with saline
  2. Examine immediately (within 10 minutes)
  3. Look for motile, flagellated organisms
  4. Trichomonads are pear-shaped, 10-20 μm
Critical: Sensitivity decreases to 20% after 1 hour - immediate examination essential

Culture Methods

  • InPouch TV: Self-contained system, read daily for 5 days
  • Diamond's medium: Traditional culture method
  • Incubate at 37°C
  • More sensitive than wet mount

NAAT Testing

Preferred Method: NAAT is recommended for all symptomatic women and high-risk populations
  • Can test vaginal, endocervical, or urine specimens
  • Some assays FDA-approved for male urine
  • Allows for batching and automation

Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV) Diagnosis

Overview

  • Two types: HSV-1 and HSV-2
  • Both can cause genital and oral infections
  • Lifelong infection with recurrences
  • Can be transmitted during asymptomatic shedding
  • Clinical diagnosis often unreliable

When to Test

Direct Detection (Lesions)

  • Active vesicles/ulcers
  • Atypical lesions
  • Confirm clinical diagnosis
  • Type-specific identification

Serology (No Lesions)

  • Recurrent symptoms
  • Partner with HSV
  • HIV-positive patients
  • NOT for screening

Slide 22 of 45

HSV: Direct Detection Methods

Specimen Collection

  • Unroof vesicle with needle/scalpel
  • Absorb vesicle fluid with swab
  • Vigorously swab base of ulcer
  • Place in viral transport medium

Detection Methods

PCR (Preferred)

  • Most sensitive method
  • HSV-1 vs HSV-2
  • Quantitative available
Sensitivity: >95%

Viral Culture

  • 100% specific
  • Lower sensitivity
  • Typing possible
Sensitivity: 50-70%

DFA

  • Rapid results
  • Requires expertise
  • Less sensitive
Sensitivity: 60-70%
Timing Matters: Sensitivity decreases as lesions heal - test early lesions

HSV: Serological Testing

Type-Specific Serology

  • Detects IgG antibodies to HSV-1 and HSV-2
  • Based on glycoprotein G (gG-1 and gG-2)
  • Takes 12 weeks to develop antibodies
  • Cannot determine infection site

Available Tests

Test Type Performance Comments
HerpeSelect EIA Sens: 96-100%
Spec: 96-100%		 Laboratory-based
Western Blot Gold standard Research only
Point-of-care Variable Not recommended
Caution: Positive serology does not indicate active infection or infectivity

Interpretation Challenges

  • False positives in low-risk populations
  • Consider confirmatory testing for low-positive results
  • Cannot distinguish oral from genital infection

Human Papillomavirus (HPV) Diagnosis

Overview

  • >150 HPV types, ~40 affect genital area
  • High-risk types: 16, 18 (70% of cervical cancers)
  • Low-risk types: 6, 11 (90% of genital warts)
  • Most infections clear spontaneously

HPV Testing Indications

Recommended

  • Cervical cancer screening
  • ASC-US triage
  • Post-treatment monitoring
  • Primary screening >30 years

NOT Recommended

  • Men (any site)
  • Genital wart diagnosis
  • Routine STI screening
  • Women <30 years (alone)
Key Point: HPV testing is for cervical cancer prevention, not STI diagnosis

Mycoplasma genitalium Diagnosis

Clinical Significance

  • Causes 15-20% of NGU in men
  • Associated with PID in women
  • High rates of antibiotic resistance
  • Often persistent/recurrent

Diagnostic Approach

Testing Indications

  • Persistent/recurrent urethritis
  • Persistent/recurrent cervicitis
  • PID evaluation
  • Contact of positive case

Laboratory Methods

  • NAAT only - cannot be cultured routinely
  • FDA-approved assays now available
  • Resistance testing recommended if available
  • Specimens: First-void urine, vaginal/cervical swabs
Important: Macrolide resistance >50% - resistance-guided therapy preferred

Slide 27 of 45

Bacterial Vaginosis Diagnosis

Overview

  • Polymicrobial dysbiosis - not single pathogen
  • Loss of lactobacilli, overgrowth of anaerobes
  • Most common cause of vaginal discharge
  • Associated with STI acquisition, pregnancy complications

Diagnostic Methods

Amsel Criteria (3 of 4)

  • Thin, homogeneous discharge
  • pH >4.5
  • Positive whiff test
  • Clue cells on microscopy

Nugent Score (Gram stain)

  • 0-3: Normal
  • 4-6: Intermediate
  • 7-10: BV
  • Gold standard method

NAAT Tests

  • BD Affirm, BD Max vaginal panel
  • Detect BV-associated bacteria
  • Good correlation with Nugent score

Case Study 1: Syphilis Diagnosis

Clinical Scenario

Patient: 28-year-old male

Presentation: Painless genital ulcer for 1 week

History: New sexual partner 3 weeks ago

Laboratory Approach

  1. Direct detection: Swab ulcer for darkfield or PCR
  2. Serology: RPR and TPPA
  3. Additional: HIV, HSV PCR (rule out)

Results

  • Darkfield: Positive for spirochetes
  • RPR: Reactive 1:32
  • TPPA: Positive
  • HIV: Negative
Diagnosis: Primary syphilis
Action: Treat, partner notification, follow-up serology at 6 & 12 months

Slide 36 of 45

Case Study 2: Persistent Urethritis

Clinical Scenario

Patient: 25-year-old male

Presentation: Persistent urethral discharge after treatment

History: Treated for gonorrhea 3 weeks ago

Initial Testing

  • CT/NG NAAT: Both negative
  • Gram stain: WBCs, no organisms

Additional Testing Indicated

  1. M. genitalium NAAT - Common cause of persistent NGU
  2. T. vaginalis NAAT - Can cause urethritis in men
  3. HSV PCR - If any lesions

Results & Management

  • M. genitalium: Positive
  • Resistance testing: Macrolide resistant
Treatment: Moxifloxacin-based regimen due to resistance

Laboratory Troubleshooting

Common Issues and Solutions

Problem Possible Causes Solutions
NAAT inhibition Blood, mucus, preservatives Dilute specimen, use different platform
Culture contamination Poor technique, transport delay Training, selective media, rapid transport
Discordant serology Early infection, false positive Repeat testing, alternative methods
Invalid controls Storage, expiration, technique Check storage, new lot, retrain staff

When to Reject Specimens

  • Unlabeled or mislabeled
  • Wrong transport medium
  • Excessive transport time
  • Visible contamination
  • Insufficient quantity
Best Practice: Communicate with clinical team before rejecting irreplaceable specimens

Cost-Effective Testing Strategies

Optimizing Test Utilization

Avoid Overuse

  • HSV serology screening
  • Repeat treponemal tests
  • Culture when NAAT available
  • Too frequent retesting

Don't Miss

  • Extragenital testing
  • Syphilis in pregnancy
  • Resistance testing
  • Partner testing

Cost-Effectiveness Strategies

  • Pooled testing: For low-prevalence screening
  • Reflex testing: Automatic confirmatory tests
  • Syndromic panels: When multiple pathogens likely
  • Risk-based screening: Target high-risk populations
Remember: Preventing one case of PID or congenital syphilis saves more than hundreds of tests cost

Key Laboratory Messages

Critical Points to Remember

  1. NAAT is preferred for CT, NG, TV, MG when available
  2. Syphilis requires two tests - treponemal + non-treponemal
  3. Culture is essential for antimicrobial resistance monitoring
  4. Specimen quality determines test performance
  5. Extragenital testing based on sexual practices

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Using treponemal tests to monitor syphilis treatment
  • Testing too soon after exposure (window period)
  • Ignoring pre-analytical requirements
  • Not confirming positive screening tests
  • Poor communication with clinicians
Excellence in STI testing requires continuous education and quality improvement
