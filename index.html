Why Accurate Laboratory Diagnosis Matters

The Critical Role of Laboratory Testing

  • STIs are a major public health concern globally and in Saudi Arabia
  • Many STIs are asymptomatic - laboratory testing is crucial
  • Accurate diagnosis enables:
    • Appropriate treatment selection
    • Prevention of complications
    • Partner notification and treatment
    • Surveillance and public health planning
Key Statistics:

Up to 80% of chlamydia and gonorrhea infections in women are asymptomatic

Remember:

Clinical diagnosis alone is unreliable for most STIs - laboratory confirmation is essential

Specimen Collection: Critical First Step

Types of Specimens

  • Urogenital: Urine, urethral, cervical, vaginal swabs
  • Extragenital: Pharyngeal, rectal swabs
  • Blood: Serum, plasma, whole blood
  • Lesions: Ulcer swabs, vesicle fluid
Critical:

Improper specimen collection is the most common cause of false-negative results

Key Collection Principles

Specimen Key Points
First-void urine First 10-20 mL, no urination for ≥1 hour
Vaginal swab Self-collected or clinician, preferred for NAAT
Cervical swab Remove excess mucus first, rotate 360°
Ulcer specimen Clean with saline, sample base of ulcer

Laboratory Quality Management

Three Phases of Quality

Pre-analytical (70% of errors)

  • Proper patient identification
  • Correct specimen collection
  • Appropriate transport conditions
  • Complete requisition forms

Analytical (10% of errors)

  • Daily quality control
  • Equipment calibration
  • Standard operating procedures
  • Staff competency

Post-analytical (20% of errors)

  • Result verification
  • Timely reporting
  • Clear interpretation
  • Critical value notification
Quality Indicators:

Monitor specimen rejection rate (<2%), turnaround time, and critical value reporting time

Internal Quality Control

Essential Elements

Component Frequency Action
Positive/Negative Controls Each run Document results
Temperature monitoring Daily Record, alert if out
Reagent checks Before use Check expiry
Equipment maintenance Per schedule Preventive care

Westgard Rules

  • 1-3s: Warning - one control exceeds 3 SD
  • 2-2s: Reject - two consecutive controls exceed 2 SD
  • R-4s: Reject - range between controls exceeds 4 SD
Never report patient results if QC fails!

Syphilis: Treponema pallidum

Key Characteristics

  • Spirochete bacterium - cannot be cultured routinely
  • Transmission: Sexual contact, vertical, blood
  • Clinical stages: Primary, secondary, latent, tertiary
  • Great imitator - diverse clinical manifestations

Clinical Stages and Testing

Stage Time Clinical Features Tests
Primary 3-90 days Painless chancre Direct detection + Serology
Secondary 4-10 weeks Rash, systemic Serology (high titers)
Latent Variable Asymptomatic Serology only
Tertiary Years Gummas, CVS, neuro Serology + CSF
Slide 12 of 45

Syphilis: Direct Detection Methods

Available Methods

Method Sensitivity Specificity Comments
Darkfield Microscopy 80% 97% Requires expertise, immediate exam
DFA-TP 90% 99% Not for oral lesions
PCR 95% 99% Not widely available
IHC 70% 95% For tissue specimens

Specimen Collection for Direct Detection

  1. Clean lesion with saline (not antiseptic)
  2. Abrade surface gently to induce serous exudate
  3. Collect clear fluid (not blood or pus)
  4. For darkfield: Examine immediately
  5. For DFA/PCR: Use appropriate transport
Limitation:

Direct detection only useful for active lesions in primary and secondary syphilis

Two Categories of Tests

Non-treponemal Tests

  • RPR (Rapid Plasma Reagin)
  • VDRL (Venereal Disease Research Lab)

  • Monitor treatment response

Treponemal Tests

  • TPPA/TPHA
  • FTA-ABS
  • EIA/CIA

Syphilis Testing Algorithms

Traditional Algorithm

  1. Screen with non-treponemal test (RPR/VDRL)
  2. If positive → Confirm with treponemal test (TPPA)
  3. Report with titer (e.g., RPR 1:32, TPPA positive)

Reverse Algorithm

  1. Screen with treponemal test (EIA/CIA)
  2. If positive → Perform non-treponemal test (RPR)
  3. If discordant → Second treponemal test (TPPA)

Result Interpretation

Treponemal Non-treponemal Interpretation
Positive Positive Active syphilis (confirm stage)
Positive Negative Past treated syphilis OR early/late syphilis
Negative Positive False positive (confirm with another treponemal)
Slide 15 of 45

Monitoring Syphilis Treatment

Key Principles

  • Use quantitative non-treponemal tests (RPR/VDRL)
  • 4-fold decline in titer = successful treatment
  • Treponemal tests remain positive - do NOT use for monitoring

Follow-up Schedule

Stage Follow-up Expected
Primary/Secondary 6, 12 months 4-fold by 6-12 mo
Early Latent 6, 12, 24 months 4-fold by 12-24 mo
Late Latent 6, 12, 24 months May decline slowly
Neurosyphilis Every 6 months CSF + serology
Treatment Failure:

No 4-fold decline or clinical signs persist → Re-evaluate, consider neurosyphilis, retreat

Gonorrhea: Neisseria gonorrhoeae

Key Characteristics

  • Gram-negative intracellular diplococcus
  • Affects urogenital, pharyngeal, and rectal sites
  • Often asymptomatic, especially in women (>50%)
  • Can cause PID, infertility, disseminated infection

Clinical Presentations

Site Symptoms Complications
Male urethra Purulent discharge Epididymitis
Female cervix Often asymptomatic PID, infertility
Pharynx Usually asymptomatic Transmission
Rectum Discharge, pain Proctitis
Antimicrobial Resistance Crisis:

Rising resistance to all first-line antibiotics - culture for susceptibility testing is critical

Slide 17 of 45

Gonorrhea: Microscopy

Gram Stain Method

  1. Prepare smear from discharge/swab
  2. Heat fix gently
  3. Gram stain procedure
  4. Look for intracellular Gram-negative diplococci in PMNs

Performance by Site

Site Sensitivity Specificity Use
Male urethral 95% 99% Recommended
Cervical 50% 95% Limited value
Pharyngeal <20% Poor Not recommended
Rectal 40% Poor Not recommended
Important:

Gram stain is only reliable for symptomatic male urethral discharge

Slide 18 of 45

Gonorrhea: Culture Methods

Culture Requirements

  • Media: Chocolate agar, Thayer-Martin, MTM
  • Atmosphere: 5-10% CO₂, humid
  • Temperature: 35-37°C
  • Transport: Immediate plating or CO₂ transport

Identification Steps

  1. Gray, mucoid colonies on chocolate agar
  2. Oxidase positive
  3. Catalase positive
  4. Gram-negative diplococci
  5. Sugar utilization: Glucose only
Critical Advantage:

Culture allows antimicrobial susceptibility testing - essential for resistance surveillance

Limitations

  • Organism is fragile - dies quickly
  • Lower sensitivity than NAAT (70-85%)
  • Requires expertise and resources
  • Takes 24-72 hours
Slide 19 of 45

Gonorrhea: Nucleic Acid Testing

NAAT Advantages

  • Superior sensitivity (>95%) compared to culture
  • Can test multiple anatomical sites
  • Stable specimens - no viability required
  • Automated platforms available
  • Co-testing with Chlamydia trachomatis

Specimen Types for NAAT

Patient Preferred Notes
Males First-void urine First 10-20 mL
Females Vaginal swab Self or clinician
Extragenital Site swab Based on practices
Limitation:

NAAT cannot provide antimicrobial susceptibility - culture still needed for resistance testing

Gonorrhea: Antimicrobial Resistance

Global Resistance Crisis

  • Resistance to all first-line antibiotics has emerged
  • Penicillin, tetracycline, fluoroquinolones - widespread
  • Azithromycin resistance increasing
  • Ceftriaxone is last remaining empiric option
  • XDR (extensively drug-resistant) strains reported

Susceptibility Testing Methods

Method Use Comments
Disk Diffusion Routine CLSI guidelines
E-test MIC More precise
Agar Dilution Reference Gold standard
Saudi Recommendation:

Culture from treatment failures and perform susceptibility testing. Report to surveillance.

Chlamydia trachomatis

Key Characteristics

  • Obligate intracellular bacterium
  • Most common bacterial STI worldwide
  • 70-80% asymptomatic in women, 50% in men
  • Major cause of PID, infertility, ectopic pregnancy
  • Includes LGV serovars (L1, L2, L3)

Clinical Syndromes

Syndrome Serovars Features
Urogenital infection D-K Urethritis, cervicitis, PID
Lymphogranuloma venereum L1, L2, L3 Genital ulcer, lymphadenopathy
Trachoma A, B, C Eye infection (not STI)
Neonatal D-K Conjunctivitis, pneumonia

Chlamydia: Diagnostic Methods

Evolution of Testing

  • Historical: Cell culture (McCoy cells) - Complex
  • Transitional: DFA, EIA - Lower sensitivity
  • Current Standard: NAAT - Highly sensitive

NAAT Performance

Parameter Value
Sensitivity >90-95%
Specificity >99%
Detection limit 1-10 organisms
FDA-approved sites Multiple
Key Point:

NAAT is the only recommended method for routine chlamydia diagnosis

Chlamydia: Special Considerations

LGV (Lymphogranuloma Venereum)

  • Caused by C. trachomatis serovars L1, L2, L3
  • Requires genotyping - not available in routine labs
  • Clinical diagnosis + positive chlamydia NAAT
  • Consider in: proctitis, inguinal buboes, genital ulcers
  • Treatment differs from non-LGV chlamydia

Test of Cure

Recommendation Rationale
NOT routine Dead organism detection
Wait ≥4 weeks Minimize false positives
Indicated for: • Pregnant women
• Compliance concerns
• Persistent symptoms
Important:

Retest all patients at 3 months to detect reinfection (not test of cure)

Trichomonas vaginalis

Key Facts

  • Flagellated protozoan parasite
  • Most common non-viral STI globally
  • 70-85% asymptomatic
  • Associated with:
    • Adverse pregnancy outcomes
    • Increased HIV transmission (2-3x)
    • PID risk

Clinical Manifestations

Population Symptoms
Women Frothy discharge, vulvar irritation
Men Usually asymptomatic
Complications Preterm birth, low birth weight
Public Health Impact:

Often overlooked but has significant morbidity and facilitates HIV transmission

Trichomoniasis: Diagnostic Methods

Diagnostic Methods Comparison

Method Sensitivity Advantages Limitations
Wet Mount 44-68% Rapid, inexpensive Requires immediate exam
Culture 75-96% Viable organisms 3-7 days, special media
NAAT >95% Highly sensitive Cost, availability
Rapid Antigen 82-95% Point-of-care Moderate sensitivity

Wet Mount Technique

  1. Mix vaginal discharge with saline
  2. Examine immediately (within 10 minutes)
  3. Look for motile, flagellated organisms
  4. Trichomonads are pear-shaped, 10-20 μm
Critical:

Sensitivity decreases to 20% after 1 hour - immediate examination essential

Slide 27 of 45

Trichomoniasis: Culture and NAAT

Culture Methods

  • InPouch TV: Self-contained system, read daily for 5 days
  • Diamond's medium: Traditional culture method
  • Incubate at 37°C
  • More sensitive than wet mount
  • Allows for susceptibility testing if needed

NAAT Advantages

  • Superior sensitivity (>95%)
  • Can test vaginal, endocervical, or urine specimens
  • Some assays FDA-approved for male urine
  • Stable specimens
  • Batching possible
Recommendation:

NAAT is preferred for all symptomatic women and high-risk populations when available

Testing in Men

  • Historically challenging
  • NAAT from urine now available
  • Consider testing male partners

Herpes Simplex Virus

Key Characteristics

  • Two types: HSV-1 and HSV-2
  • Both can cause genital and oral infections
  • Lifelong infection with recurrences
  • Can be transmitted during asymptomatic shedding
  • Clinical diagnosis often unreliable

When to Test

Direct Detection (Lesions)

  • Active vesicles/ulcers
  • Atypical lesions
  • Confirm clinical diagnosis
  • Type-specific identification

Serology (No Lesions)

  • Recurrent symptoms
  • Partner with HSV
  • HIV-positive patients
  • NOT for screening

HSV: Direct Detection Methods

Specimen Collection

  1. Unroof vesicle with needle/scalpel
  2. Absorb vesicle fluid with swab
  3. Vigorously swab base of ulcer
  4. Place in viral transport medium

Detection Methods

Method Sensitivity Advantages
PCR >95% HSV-1 vs HSV-2
Culture 50-70% 100% specific
DFA 60-70% Rapid results
Timing Matters:

Sensitivity decreases as lesions heal - test vesicles and early ulcers for best results

Human Papillomavirus (HPV)

Key Facts

  • >150 HPV types, ~40 affect genital area
  • High-risk types: 16, 18 cause 70% of cervical cancers
  • Low-risk types: 6, 11 cause 90% of genital warts
  • Most infections clear spontaneously within 2 years
  • Persistent HR-HPV → cancer risk

HPV Testing Indications

Recommended NOT Recommended
• Cervical cancer screening
• ASC-US triage
• Post-treatment monitoring
• Primary screening >30 years 		• Men (any site)
• Genital wart diagnosis
• Routine STI screening
• Women <30 years (alone)
Key Point:

HPV testing is for cervical cancer prevention, not STI diagnosis

Other STI Pathogens

Mycoplasma genitalium

  • Causes 15-20% of NGU in men
  • Associated with PID in women
  • High rates of antibiotic resistance
  • Testing: NAAT only
  • Important: Macrolide resistance >50%

Bacterial Vaginosis

  • Polymicrobial dysbiosis
  • Amsel Criteria (3 of 4):
    • Thin discharge
    • pH >4.5
    • Positive whiff test
    • Clue cells >20%
  • Nugent Score: 7-10 = BV

Chancroid (H. ducreyi)

  • Rare in developed countries
  • Culture: Special media, <80% sensitive
  • PCR available in research settings

Case Study 1: Syphilis Diagnosis

Clinical Scenario

  • Patient: 28-year-old male
  • Presentation: Painless genital ulcer for 1 week
  • History: New sexual partner 3 weeks ago

Laboratory Approach

  1. Direct detection: Swab ulcer for darkfield or PCR
  2. Serology: RPR and TPPA
  3. Additional: HIV, HSV PCR (rule out)

Results

Test Result
Darkfield microscopy Positive for spirochetes
RPR Reactive 1:32
TPPA Positive
HIV Negative
Diagnosis: Primary syphilis
Treatment: Benzathine penicillin G 2.4 MU IM
Follow-up: RPR at 6 and 12 months
Slide 39 of 45

Case Study 2: Persistent Urethritis

Clinical Scenario

  • Patient: 25-year-old male
  • Presentation: Persistent urethral discharge after treatment
  • History: Treated for gonorrhea 3 weeks ago

Initial Testing

  • CT/NG NAAT: Both negative
  • Gram stain: WBCs, no organisms

Additional Testing

  1. M. genitalium NAAT - Common cause of persistent NGU
  2. T. vaginalis NAAT - Can cause urethritis in men
  3. HSV PCR - If any lesions

Results & Management

  • M. genitalium: Positive
  • Resistance testing: Macrolide resistant
  • Treatment: Moxifloxacin 400mg daily × 7 days
  • Partner notification: Essential
Key Learning:

Consider M. genitalium in persistent NGU. Resistance is common - consider resistance-guided therapy.

Laboratory Troubleshooting

Common Issues and Solutions

Problem Possible Causes Solutions
NAAT inhibition Blood, mucus, preservatives Dilute specimen, use different platform
Culture contamination Poor technique, transport delay Training, selective media, rapid transport
Discordant serology Early infection, false positive Repeat testing, alternative methods
Invalid controls Storage, expiration, technique Check storage, new lot, retrain staff

When to Reject Specimens

  • Unlabeled or mislabeled
  • Wrong transport medium
  • Excessive transport time
  • Visible contamination
  • Insufficient quantity
Best Practice:

Communicate with clinical team before rejecting irreplaceable specimens

Key Laboratory Messages

Critical Points to Remember

  1. NAAT is preferred for CT, NG, TV, MG when available
  2. Syphilis requires two tests - treponemal + non-treponemal
  3. Culture is essential for antimicrobial resistance monitoring
  4. Specimen quality determines test performance
  5. Extragenital testing based on sexual practices

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Using treponemal tests to monitor syphilis treatment
  • Testing too soon after exposure (window period)
  • Ignoring pre-analytical requirements
  • Not confirming positive screening tests
  • Poor communication with clinicians
Excellence in STI testing requires continuous education and quality improvement
